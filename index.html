What a Trump Presidency Means for Immigrant Youth

By Brandon Shaik

Republican president-elect Donald Trump has built a platform on anti-immigration policies — threatening to build a wall and deport millions. All of his propositions are a stark contrast to the efforts of President Obama to provide undocumented youth with a chance at the American Dream.

In June 2012 the Obama Administration passed an Executive Action called Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA), a policy which provides a renewable two-year work permit and exemption from deportation to individuals who entered the U.S. illegally before their sixteenth birthday and before June 2007. The policy does not provide a pathway to citizenship for these individuals but it does provide non-immigrant legal status.

Donald Trump has promised his supporters that, should he be elected, he will end the program - deporting hundreds of thousands of college students and children

Since the start of the program, a total of 1.5 million applications were received, of which 1.2 million have been approved. A recent study in the Journal of Public Economics found that DACA has moved between 50,000 and 75,000 immigrants into the labor force and decreased unemployment while also increasing personal income for those in the bottom of the income distribution.

In 2014, President Obama announced a series of changes to DACA in an effort to clamp down on illegal immigration, deport felons rather than families and require criminal background checks for all undocumented immigrants.

These changes included expanding the program to allow parents of U.S. Citizens or permanent residents to request deferred action, extending provisional waivers of unlawful presence to spouses and children of legal permanent residents, and modernizing immigrant and nonimmigrant visa programs.

As a result of these provisions, fewer DACA requests were approved in 2014, and a large amount of requests remained pending at the end of the year.

Following Mr. Trump's win on Tuesday, many fear that he will end DACA and deport them to their home countries.

“I’m always scared because at any moment, DACA can be taken away from us,” Colombian immigrant Kemberley Gil told Fusion. “My life, my future, the future of my mom is in these people’s hands, and they don’t really care, and they don’t see the lives that they’re affecting.”
